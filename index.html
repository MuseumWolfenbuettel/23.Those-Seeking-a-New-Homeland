23.Those Seeking a New Homeland

As a result of Nazi Germany's aggressive policies, millions of Germans lost their homes starting in 1944/45. Increasing numbers of refugees and expellees from former German eastern territories, many of them starving, exhausted, and traumatized, arrived in Wolfenbüttel. Here, they encountered foreigners—most of them former forced laborers—who, after their liberation, were classified as Displaced Persons (DPs). These individuals awaited repatriation or resettlement in third countries and gradually left Wolfenbüttel over time. The number of refugees and expellees in Wolfenbüttel peaked on October 1, 1948, at 11,462 individuals, accounting for nearly 30 percent of the town’s 38,804 inhabitants. Forced accommodations, cramped living conditions, and the necessity of sharing kitchens and bathrooms led to conflicts between long-time Wolfenbüttel residents and the newcomers, who primarily came from Silesia and East Prussia. Many locals in Wolfenbüttel viewed the Germans from the east with suspicion and, at times, outright hostility. The significant influx of Catholics also introduced unfamiliar religious differences to the predominantly Protestant community. Diverging values and cultural traditions tested the cohesion of the community. Wolfenbüttel became a shared new homeland only after the period of economic scarcity was overcome and mutual resentments gradually dissolved over time.
